A friend of mine — a GP at a top-tier B2B SaaS VC — once told me something that stuck:

> "A CEO should spend 20% of their time on corporate development."

At first, it sounded like one of those typical VC mantras. Sensible. Tactical. Nothing revolutionary. But the more I thought about it, the more it stayed with me.

It was one of those deceptively simple ideas that grows heavier each time you pick it up. What began as a pragmatic tip started revealing deeper layers of truth. I found myself going over it again and again.

And it makes sense. In Europe, **M&A is by far the dominant exit route** for VC-backed companies. IPOs are rare. So, if acquisition is the most likely outcome, corporate development isn't just a last-minute activity — it's a mindset to adopt early.

At its core, corporate development is about **getting on the radar of potential buyers**. It means building relationships with key executives, understanding where they are in their strategic roadmap, identifying pain points, and positioning the company — through commercial or technological partnerships — as the answer to those pains.

In essence, it's about **building trust and familiarity long before the idea of a transaction is even on the table**. It gives the leverage when the time comes to explore strategic options.

This brings up a question I get asked often: When is the time right?

And my answer — *the best time to exit is when you're growing* — almost always triggers discomfort. It feels like a betrayal. And I deeply understand.

Growth is when things are finally working. You've built a great team, you've found product-market fit, and you've secured investor support to build something big. Selling at that point feels like **giving up early** — especially if your goal was to build a **category-defining company**.

But here's the paradox: **growth gives you leverage — and leverage gives you options.**

When you're growing, it feels like the assumptions about the opportunity you are pursuing are right:

- TAM is real
- The product resonates
- The go-to-market engine seems repeatable
- The team is executing well.

This creates a halo effect. Everyone's energized. But if even one assumption is off, you eventually hit a ceiling.

And in SaaS, ceilings rarely reveal themselves dramatically. The signs are often subtle: sales cycles lengthen, CAC quietly increases, expansion slows, pipeline quality weakens…

But because topline is still up, it's easy to overlook those signals — until they become irreversible.

The best founders I've worked with all share a similar trait: **they're a little paranoid.**

Or they surround themselves with board members who are analytically rigorous and deeply pattern-aware:

- They instill a **culture of data literacy**.
- They know the metrics that matter, and they're trained to detect when lagging indicators are masking deeper issues with the **growth runway**.
- They encourage a mindset where founders are constantly **stress-testing their assumptions** — not out of fear, but out of discipline.
- They create space for doubt — and doubt, when handled well, becomes a gift. It puts these founders in a mindset of testing hypotheses and exploring strategic options **proactively**, not reactively.

That's exactly when corporate development becomes a strategic asset.

Because, if you've already built relationships with potential buyers **while you're still growing**, you're not navigating from a place of weakness — you're operating from a position of strength.

Strategic acquisitions don't happen overnight. They're driven by internal champions — executives who see the fit, believe in the upside, and are willing to make the case internally. But that case isn't made in isolation.

I've written before that champions have to **justify the timing, the strategic rationale, and the valuation** to their board. And this is where your growth becomes their greatest asset.

Growth tells a compelling story — not just about past performance, but about future potential. It gives your internal champion the credibility to say:

> "This team is winning. We need to act now before someone else does."

But not all growth is created equal. The growth that matters is **relative to your peers and your size**. Here's a rough benchmark to keep in mind:

According to Bessemer Venture Partners, the long-term average revenue multiple for public SaaS companies is 6x ARR. To justify that multiple:

- Companies below €3M ARR should be growing at least 50%
- Companies between €3–10M ARR need 40%+
- Companies above €10M ARR should be growing 30%+

The faster you grow beyond that baseline, the more the internal champion can justify paying a **premium**. You make it **easier for them to push the deal through**.

*And in some cases, it creates what I call a local maximum — a moment where your performance relative to peers makes you look like the next category leader.*

That's when valuations break from logic and reflect momentum:

- High-growth companies in the €1–3M range can reach €50–100M valuations.
- Fast-growing companies above €10M can be priced as category killers — based on a multiple of their last round.

In conclusion, the best time to exit is when you have doubt — not fear — about your growth runway.

When in doubt, the relationships you've built gives you the optionality to not exit prematurely, but to exit intelligently, at the right moment, and on the right terms.
