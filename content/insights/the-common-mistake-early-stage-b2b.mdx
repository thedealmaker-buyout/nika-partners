## The Pattern Recognition of a Mentor

I love rewatching movies because, as my perspective evolves, I often uncover new takeaways I hadn't noticed before. Over the last Christmas break, I revisited *The Hunger Games* and found myself particularly struck by Haymitch's character.

What impressed me wasn't just his cynicism or survival instincts, but his **pattern recognition** — the ability to anticipate deadly mistakes and guide the heroes away from them. His wisdom didn't come from raw intelligence alone; it came from **exposure**.

As a former Victor, he had survived the Games himself. But more importantly, as a mentor, he had seen dozens of tributes go through the same experience, recognizing the recurring pitfalls that separated **survival from failure**. His advantage wasn't just that he had played the game — it was that he had watched others play it over and over again.

After being exposed to dozens of early-stage B2B SaaS M&A situations, I've developed a similar kind of pattern recognition.

> *One of the most recurring mistakes I see is that companies targeting mid-market to enterprise customers often **position themselves as a Product, while they are a Feature.***

- **Product:** a comprehensive solution solving a mission-critical problem
- **Feature:** a niche tool solving a narrow problem

This pattern is natural, as a Product is perceived as having **higher strategic value** in an M&A context, making it more attractive to buyers and commanding a higher valuation multiple than a Feature, which is often seen as an add-on.

## The Problem with Inflated Positioning

The problem with **inflated positioning** is that it sets us up for failure in building rapport with potential buyers — especially those who have the ability to scale a company by **distributing the product to their large customer base**.

I've written before that for strategic buyers an acquisition that doesn't generate at least a 10% impact on the current fiscal year is typically viewed as an investment. The core thesis of such an investment lies in the management team of the target, as they will ultimately be responsible for executing the synergies that justify the deal.

> *Simply put: the more a buyer values the management team, the more likely they are to make an offer.*

In contrast, a sub-scale product (below $5M ARR) without a strong management team to drive synergies post-acquisition holds little value. By default, buyers perceive a sub-scale product as a point solution.

> If we **pitch it as a strategic product**, we **immediately lose credibility** — and with it, our chance of ever receiving an offer from that buyer.

However, if we first acknowledge the product's feature-like nature, we establish common ground with the buyer. From there, we can introduce the strategic synergies.

## How to Spot a Feature vs. a Product

I've learned to quickly spot when a company is a Feature rather than a Product by recognizing these recurring patterns:

- Growth stalls after $2M ARR and flattens at $5M.
- The biggest competitor is the status quo.
- Prospects remain stuck in the pipeline because internal champions struggle to secure executive buy-in.
- Approval from CFO, Procurement, or IT is a major hurdle, delaying or blocking deals.
- Even with a signed team-level purchase order, IT prevents deployment.
- Implementation drags on for 6 to 18 months, making adoption slow and painful.
- Churn spikes when discretionary budgets shrink.
- The top 10 customers make up 50% of revenue.

When these signs appear, it's a strong indication that the company is a Feature — a critical distinction that affects M&A positioning.

## The Right M&A Positioning for Features

I've written before that one of the most effective ways to market a Feature to strategic buyers is to position the status quo as a significantly riskier alternative than acquiring the target's assets. This tactic works particularly well for companies with a **critical mass of customers** (100+).

For companies without that scale, M&A success depends on identifying alternative categories of buyers through refined **buyer segmentation** — a topic for another post!
