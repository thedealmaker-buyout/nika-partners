## The Power Law of Venture Capital

Last year, David Clark from VenCap International — an LP with a mature portfolio of established venture funds — broke the Internet by sharing historical data on their investments, both realized and unrealized.

This dataset, covering **11,350 companies** backed by 259 venture capital firms between 1986 and 2018, revealed:

- Around 1 in 9 companies returned at least 5x the capital invested.
- Only 121 out of the 11,350 managed to return 100% or more of the committed capital of the fund that backed them.

These takeaways reinforce the power-law-driven nature of venture capital business, underscoring why VCs concentrate their capital, resources, and attention on the **most successful startups**.

> Today, successful startups are defined by their ability to scale from $1M ARR to $100M ARR, becoming a "centaur".

Bessemer Venture Partners, a leading growth investor with a portfolio of over 200 SaaS investments, has established benchmarks for **the time it should take** to become a centaur:

- **Good**: 12 years
- **Better**: 7 years
- **Best**: 6 years.

If a company secures $5M in Series A funding at a $20M valuation but fails to **achieve the growth** needed to reach centaur benchmarks on schedule, the company should first focus on making the business sustainable, then exploring exit options.

## When VCs Rely on Growth Narratives

On an exit, VCs get paid first through their liquidation preference. However, as I've written before, VCs have an incentive to ensure that founders make money, even on a downgraded scenario.

This means that any deal in which VCs choose to **take their preference instead of converting to common shares** alongside the founders, without compromising their own interests, is fundamentally **a bad deal**.

So, if the $5M investment fails to create a business with an Enterprise Value of $25M under standard market conditions, VCs are incentivized to **rely on growth narratives to attract M&A buyers who are willing to pay a premium.**

> When a deal depends heavily on storytelling to justify a premium, M&A turns into a high-stakes value communication exercise.

From my experience, the primary reason this exercise often fails is that the growth narrative **lacks the right data** needed to make the story credible.

## The "Ready for Scale" Problem

Most of the growth stories I've heard are based on the assumption that the VC-backed company has **a solid product but hasn't yet reached the scale** needed to fully exploit its potential. Therefore, a buyer with robust distribution channels could **unlock significant value** by scaling the company's product.

When dealing with these "ready for scale" growth narratives, I've found that the primary question buyers ask **themselves** is not simply whether they can distribute the product but **why it is worth** distributing at all.

## Why GRR Matters More Than NRR

Churn is a powerful indicator of a product's strength, yet many VC-backed companies attempt to justify their product strength through Net Dollar Retention (NRR), which is the weakest churn indicator of product stickiness.

By factoring in upsells, NRR can cover a lot of sins. Strong customer success and upsell operations can compensate for **gaps in a product's stickiness or its mission-critical role**.

> *That's why Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) stands out as the primary indicator of how strong a product truly is.*

## A Real-World Example

For example, we worked with a VC-backed company selling software to mid-market customers that was confident it could attract buyers with a "ready for scale" story. The company highlighted its 109% NRR, which falls within the benchmark for a healthy SaaS business.

In the meantime, the company's 42% GRR — far below the 85%-90% range typical for sticky products — revealed a significant underlying issue with the product. Therefore, pursuing a "ready for scale" narrative would likely have resulted in a bad deal, as buyers would quickly uncover the retention challenges.

Instead, we reframed the narrative to emphasize the company's strengths: its highly effective customer success and upsell operations. By positioning the VC-backed company as a gateway for the buyer to successfully enter and expand in a new market, we managed to secure a premium.
